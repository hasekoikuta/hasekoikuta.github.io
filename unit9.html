Smaller animals, especially insects, are much more difficult to find, much less identify. In the 1980s, Terry Erwin of the Smithsonian Institute took every living thing from just nineteen trees in Panama. He discovered twelve hundred different types of just one kind of insect. Based on this, he calculated that there may be 30 million species of insects on earth. Others, using the same or similar data, have concluded there were 13 million or 80 million or 100 million insect types. Why is it that we have so littile idea of the true number? Part of the problem is that so many insects are so tiny. For example, your pillow is probably home to about forty thousand mites. But we did not even know that these mites existed at all until 1965. If creatures so close to us remained unnoticed for so long, it is hardly surprising that so many elsewhere are still unknown.
